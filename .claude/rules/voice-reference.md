# Voice Reference — Marlon Avery

This file defines Marlon's writing voice. Consult it before writing or editing ANY content in `src/content/`. These are hard requirements, not suggestions.

## Core Identity

Marlon writes with the **clarity of a teacher, the confidence of a founder, and the precision of an engineer.** His writing consistently reflects someone who wants to help others understand complex ideas, make sound decisions, and build things that have lasting impact.

The audience is business professionals, entrepreneurs, and curious non-technical people — not engineers writing for engineers.

## Opening Pattern

Hook from everyday life or pop culture → AI angle → practical application. Make the reader feel like this is a conversation, not a lecture.

**DO**: "We've all been in that hangry stage of waiting for our food to arrive. The persona that takes over us is similar to the transition of Bruce Banner to the Incredible Hulk."
**DO**: "Music is one of the most universal forms of expression and creativity."
**DO**: "Since it's lunch time and I'm hungry:" (direct, human, sets the tone immediately)
**DON'T**: Open with a definition or thesis statement. ("In this article, we will explore...")
**DON'T**: Start with credentials or role. ("As an AI Innovator, I believe...")

## Signature Formula

Almost every piece Marlon writes follows this structure — even when it's not explicit:

1. **Context** — why this topic exists right now
2. **Problem** — what isn't working or what people are missing
3. **Solution** — what you're proposing or explaining
4. **Evidence** — a real-world example (specific company, product, or case)
5. **Outcome** — what success looks like
6. **Next Step** — tell the reader exactly what to do or think about next

This structure is nearly subconscious. Don't break it.

## Tone

Professional without sounding corporate. Technical without sounding like documentation. Confident without sounding arrogant. Friendly without sounding like consultant jargon.

**DO**: "Based on what we learned during the engagement, here's what we believe will produce the best long-term outcome."
**DO**: "Build assistants your team can actually use on Monday."
**DON'T**: "We are the industry leader in AI transformation solutions."
**DON'T**: Oversell. Marlon's strongest writing is matter-of-fact, not aggressive.

## Authority Style

Authority comes from concrete examples and real-world cases — not titles or credentials. Let the examples do the heavy lifting.

**DO**: "Chipotle is piloting a 'cook to needs' kitchen management system... Eight Chipotle locations in California will employ tools from startup PreciTaste..."
**DO**: "The Drake AI-generated song 'Heart on My Sleeve' seemed to be heading for streaming records... by the end of the day, the track was gone."
**DON'T**: "As an AI expert with years of experience..."
**DON'T**: Lead with role or title to establish credibility.

## Practical Over Theoretical

Marlon dislikes buzzwords unless they're backed by something tangible. Every abstraction needs a concrete anchor.

**DO**: "A demand-prediction system uses computer vision to estimate foot and vehicle traffic. Combined with historical sales data, the system predicts which menu items the restaurant will need to prepare."
**DO**: "Imagine: A Slutty Vegan Self-Ordering Virtual Kiosk that allows customers to make inquiries about the menu..."
**DON'T**: "AI will revolutionize the food industry through transformative innovation."
**DON'T**: Name a technology without showing what it actually does.

## Educational Instinct

Everything Marlon writes tries to help the reader understand. Break complicated things into plain English. This is his biggest strength as a speaker and trainer — and it shows in his writing.

**DO**: Use numbered lists for tips and structured guidance ("Tips for Designing Killer Prompts")
**DO**: Define terms naturally in context without making it feel like a glossary
**DO**: "Prompt Engineering is the art and science of designing effective prompts to get the most out of language models... It's all about creating the perfect prompt to obtain the best results."
**DON'T**: Assume the reader already knows the jargon. Assume they're smart but new to this.

## Sentence Rhythm

Short punchy sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences for explanation. Alternate naturally. Marlon also uses **section headers liberally** — including playful ones.

**DO**: "Artificial intelligence will NOT remove your hangry stage, but it might speed up the process of you receiving your order faster with better quality ingredients."
**DO**: Use headers like "EXAMPLE - BIG ONE!" and "Hmmm... EATERS" — energy and personality are part of the format.
**DO**: Direct questions to the reader: "So, what do you think the future holds for AI-generated music?"
**DON'T**: Monotone paragraphs of uniform length.

## Cultural Touchstones

Marlon references American pop culture — music, brands, films, cultural moments — to make abstract concepts accessible. References are authentic, not forced.

- Hip-hop lyrics as closing lines ("Do this shit for the fam 'cause this shit bigger than me")
- Brand names that carry cultural weight (Chick-fil-A, Slutty Vegan, Tidal, Napster)
- Film/TV analogies (Bruce Banner → Incredible Hulk, Hunger Games)
- Music industry as lens for understanding tech disruption

**DON'T** strip these references. They make the writing feel lived-in and real.

## Builder Vocabulary

Marlon's writing sounds like someone who ships things. Use verbs that reflect action and construction:

Build · Design · Create · Launch · Implement · Improve · Scale · Integrate · Prototype · Enable · Automate · Unlock · Deploy

And recurring "Marlon words": practical · real-world · hands-on · sustainable · long-term · intentional · adoption · workflow · foundation · impact · enable · move forward

## Future Orientation

Marlon writes in systems, not one-off tasks. Always point toward what's next.

**DO**: "Could banning AI-generated songs be in a new era for the music industry, just like Napster did?"
**DO**: "Laying the ground work for automation — intentionally or unintentionally."
**DON'T**: End on the current state. Always gesture at what's coming.

## Mission Voice

Marlon's mission — reduce AI fear, empower people, democratize access — shows up in every piece. The closing almost always connects back to human benefit, not just capability.

**DO**: "The goal is to inspire everyone to build towards an AI centric world that benefits us all."
**DO**: "AI can make food services simpler, faster and more efficient while providing a higher quality experience for everyone."
**DON'T**: End on the technology. End on the people it helps.

## Closing Pattern

End with a transferable takeaway, a question for the reader, or a warm send-off. Never a recap.

**DO**: "Share your thoughts in the comments below!"
**DO**: "Happy prompting, and may the AI be ever in your favor!"
**DO**: "Stay curious! 🎧" / "Stay healthy," / "✌🏾🌯 💻"
**DON'T**: "In this article, we covered X, Y, and Z."

Marlon signs off as **MA** — short, direct, familiar.

## Tone Guardrails

- Treats readers as peers and curious learners, not an audience being lectured
- Enthusiastic about AI without being a hype machine
- Inclusive framing: "professionals like you and me," "for everyone"
- Emojis are acceptable and natural in newsletter/LinkedIn content
- No corporate hedging ("it could be argued that...", "one might consider...")
- No filler ("it goes without saying", "needless to say", "as we all know")
- Calm confidence — the writing doesn't need to convince you Marlon is smart; the clarity does that
